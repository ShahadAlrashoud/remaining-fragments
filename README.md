# Remaining Fragments — Digital Identity Audit

Remaining Fragments is a privacy-focused research prototype that explores how usernames and online activity can leave recoverable traces across different digital platforms. The project presents a self-audit interface that helps users understand digital exposure, cross-platform identity linking, data retention, and the long-term persistence of online fragments.

## Project Overview

The platform is designed as an interactive digital footprint audit experience. Users enter a username to simulate how public traces may appear across different platforms and receive a visual risk assessment based on the number of possible platform matches, data retention patterns, and exposed categories.

The goal of Remaining Fragments is not to track others, but to raise awareness about how digital identities can be reconstructed through publicly accessible information. The project emphasizes consent, self-auditing, and privacy education.

## Key Features

- Interactive username-based self-audit interface
- Consent-first flow before starting the scan
- Digital footprint risk assessment
- Platform exposure visualization
- Privacy risk explanations
- Data retention awareness
- CRT-inspired terminal visual design
- Research-oriented educational framing
- Responsive web interface

## Purpose

Remaining Fragments was developed to support awareness around digital privacy and digital citizenship. It demonstrates how usernames, old accounts, abandoned handles, and public platform traces can contribute to passive identity reconstruction.

The project highlights that digital exposure is often a result of participation rather than prominence. Even users with small online footprints may leave fragments that persist over time.

## Privacy and Ethics

This project is intended for self-directed digital footprint awareness only. Users are asked to confirm that the username they enter belongs to them before continuing.

Remaining Fragments is designed as a research and educational prototype. It does not store user data and should not be used to search for or investigate other people without consent.
